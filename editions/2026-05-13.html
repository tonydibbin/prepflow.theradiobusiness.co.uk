01 — News 3 stories · max 1 political

Today's top stories

  • 1
    The King opens Parliament today, with the King's Speech setting out the government's legislative programme against a turbulent political backdrop.
    State Opening goes ahead this morning following last week's local elections, in which Reform UK gained close to 1,450 council seats and took control of more than a dozen councils, while Labour shed nearly 1,500 councillors. Sir Keir Starmer is facing renewed pressure from inside his own party — but the ceremony itself will be the day's defining image, with all the usual pomp, procession and televised arrival at Westminster.
    Politics · Westminster
  • 2
    A bitter blast of cold has set a new record low for the UK in May, with the mercury falling to -6.1°C overnight in the Scottish Highlands.
    Altnaharra in Sutherland recorded the coldest May night ever logged in the UK, while Shetland was hit by heavy snow showers. Forecasters say a brief warm-up is on the way later in the week, but the cold snap has already prompted late frost warnings for growers and gardeners across northern England and Scotland, with allotment forums in full panic mode about potato crops.
    Weather · UK-wide
  • 3
    A-level and GCSE exam season gets fully under way this week, with millions of students sitting their first written papers across England, Wales and Northern Ireland.
    Schools are reporting record numbers of pupils accessing wellbeing support during exam fortnight, and the major exam boards have re-issued guidance on phone-free exam halls after a string of "smart-glasses" incidents during mock exams earlier this term. Universities, meanwhile, say they're preparing for what could be a tighter clearing window than usual once results land in August.
    Education · UK-wide
02 — Showbiz forward-looking only

What's coming up

  • 1
    Eurovision week is finally upon us, with the UK's Sam Battle — performing as Look Mum No Computer — set to take to the stage tomorrow night.
    The synth-tinkering YouTuber-turned-pop-star will perform the UK entry "Eins, Zwei, Drei" as a guest spot during Thursday's second semi-final, before the Grand Final on Saturday 16 May. The UK is automatically through to the final as a Big Four nation. Bookmakers currently have Sweden and Italy as joint favourites, with the UK floating somewhere in the mid-table — but Look Mum No Computer's homemade synth rig is being talked about as a potential dark-horse moment.
    Eurovision · This week
  • 2
    The 79th Cannes Film Festival is in full swing on the Croisette, with the Palme d'Or race officially under way and a host of huge premieres lined up over the next ten days.
    South Korean director Park Chan-wook is heading this year's jury, with Pierre Salvadori's "The Electric Kiss" opening proceedings. Among the most-anticipated films screening this week and next: John Travolta's directorial debut, a new Lynne Ramsay drama, and a remastered Pan's Labyrinth screening to mark the film's anniversary. Cannes runs through to Saturday 23 May, with the closing film "Adieu Monde Cruel" by Félix de Givry.
    Film · Cannes 12–23 May
  • 3
    "Mortal Kombat II" punches into UK cinemas this Friday — with the IMAX release backed by what Warner Bros is calling its biggest marketing push of the year.
    The sequel to the 2021 reboot has been four years in the making and is expected to open big against modest competition. Cast members are still doing the rounds on UK chat shows this week ahead of release. One to flag for any film-and-gaming-leaning shows on Friday.
    Film · Fri 15 May

04 — Newsbrief light bites · pick & mix

Quick hits

  • 1
    Brits now spend an average of 14 hours a week on streaming — more than cooking, cleaning and exercising combined.
    The biggest jump is in the over-55s, who used to be the holdouts for live telly. "Second-screening" — phone in one hand, remote in the other — means we're paying less attention to what's on than ever.
  • 2
    62% of us have lied about reading a book to look smart in front of someone we fancy.
    The most-faked titles: 1984, Pride and Prejudice and (somewhat awkwardly) the Bible. One in five admit to nodding along to an entire dinner-party conversation about a novel they'd only seen the cover of.
  • 3
    Holiday firms report a 40% spike in "shoulder season" bookings — September and October sun trips are way up on last year.
    Travellers blame the cost of school-holiday flights, unreliable summer weather, and "August feels too crowded these days". Spain, Greece and Portugal top the wish list.
  • 4
    More than half of UK workers would take a 10% pay cut to never go into the office again — but only 17% of bosses say they'd offer one.
    The gap is growing again after forced return-to-office rules. HR experts say the tension is showing up in resignation rates, particularly among under-35s.
  • 5
    Three quarters of dog owners now talk to their dog more than they talk to their partner.
    Top topics include "what we're doing later", "what we just saw out the window" and a surprising amount of Bake Off commentary. 12% admit they've apologised to the dog after a row with the human in the house.
  • 6
    Britain's most-stolen supermarket item in 2026 isn't meat or cheese — it's olive oil, with theft up 220% year on year.
    Prices have nearly doubled in two years thanks to bad harvests in Spain and Italy. Some shops have fitted security tags to single bottles; others have moved premium brands behind the counter — like cigarettes.
  • 7
    The fastest-growing hobby of 2026 is, somewhat unexpectedly, hand-embroidery — sales of needles and thread up 180% in a year.
    Crafters say it's the perfect "phone detox" — slow, tactile, satisfying. TikTok is full of 20-somethings showing off custom denim jackets. Knitting has plateaued, which knitting forums are absolutely fuming about.
  • 8
    Almost two thirds of us say we've cried at a podcast in the last year — grief, parenting and identity top the list of triggers.
    Researchers reckon it's the intimacy of audio that does it. The figure is even higher for under-30s, 71% of whom admit a recent podcast-induced tear.

07 — Facts of the Day 8 to choose from

Did you know?

  • ●
    A cloud can weigh more than a million pounds — about the same as 100 elephants. They float because they're spread out across miles of sky.
  • ●
    Honey never spoils. Edible honey has been found in 3,000-year-old Egyptian tombs and is still perfectly fine to eat.
  • ●
    Octopuses have three hearts and blue blood. Two pump blood through the gills, the third pumps it round the body.
  • ●
    The Eiffel Tower can grow up to 15cm taller in summer because the iron expands in the heat.
  • ●
    The dot above a lowercase "i" or "j" has a name — it's called a tittle.
  • ●
    Bananas are technically berries. Strawberries, somehow, are not.
  • ●
    Wombats produce cube-shaped poo, and scientists only recently worked out why — it's down to the elasticity of their intestines.
  • ●
    A "jiffy" is an actual unit of time — 1/100th of a second. So "back in a jiffy" is, technically, the fastest promise you can make.
